A Love-Hate Relationship

As far back as cave drawings, the mysterious, mischievous wolf has featured in human mythology. Is it because we see ourselves in them? Or is it because wolves are one of the few animals that might consider humans prey?

A few decades ago, there were fewer than 50 gray wolves left in the lower United States, according to most scientists. They had been hunted, trapped and poisoned to near extinction during the settling of the West.

In the mid-1990s, human attitudes changed. Or enough of them, at least, to bring back the underdog. The federal government transplanted 66 gray wolves from Canada to Yellowstone and Central Idaho to repopulate the region.

Wolves became "the environmental movement poster animal," says Doug Smith, head of the Yellowstone Wolf Project, a group created tostudy and monitor wolves in the region where they were re-introduced.

Doug Smith on the site of wolf re-introduction, Idaho.

The wolf population has since exploded. So, too, has a culture war. Today, there are nearly 1,700 gray wolves in the Northern Rockies. Good news to some humans, bad news to others -- and in between is a lot of gray.

On The Lookout

Wolves are elusive. They go where they want -- and often where they're unwanted. On the other hand, they remain ever-elusive to those looking for them. To see a wolf, let a lone photograph one, enthusiasts hire guides or go to wolf-viewing or rehabilitation centers -- which is what we did in Yellowstone National Park.

Yellowstone has become known as the place to see wild wolves. They're like celebrities, with fan clubs and even Facebook pages. For some, though, watching wolves is serious business. Scientists, rangers and organizations like the Yellowstone Wolf Project work to monitor the packs year-round.

Members of the Yellowstone Wolf Project track the park's ten wolf packs. They also help point tourists to the best places to see wolves. [A line about why they like wolves/why wolves matter/deserve protection.]

In a sense, wolves drive business. More than 3 million people visit Yellowstone National Park every year. Many to see the park's wolves. It's estimated that wolf-watching brings in roughly $35 million to the surrounding area.

The Big Bad Wolf

If there's any truth to the fables, wolves can be a menace. Watching them through binoculars in Yellowstone is one thing. But about 95 percent of wolves live outside of the park -- near people -- where they kill livestock and game animals. In 2012, wolves killed at least 67 cattle and 37 sheep in the state of Montana. And far more elk.

While some people watch wolves for fun, others like Bruce Stell are on the hunt.

Some people hunt for trophies. Others for food. In Park County, Mont., for example, the median household income is about $40,000, so hunting can be a big part of people's livelihoods. But since the re-introduction of wolves, outfitters like Hell's A-Roarin' have started leading more horseback rides than hunting trips.

A Case Of Crying Wolf?

This all raises a big question: Are wolves really to blame for the decline in elk populations? Montana Fish Wildlife and Parks is conducting a study in the Bitterroot Valley, near the Montana-Idaho border, to find out. The project got a lot of funding from area sportsmen who want to know what's killing the area's elk. [We need to know what he has concluded/found.]

Field biologist Ben Jimenez studies elk carcasses for signs of what did the killing. Radio transmitting tags were placed in the ears of 84 elk calves late last spring. Each tag emits a different radio signal that Jimenez tunes into. The signal comes to a halt if the calf is dead, which is how Jimenez knows to go find them.

The Gray Area

Wolf biologists say that at this rate, hunters couldn't eradicate wolves if they tried. They’re too elusive, too resilient. [Why were they able to the first time?] Love them or hate them, it seems that the gray wolf is here to stay.

So many ranchers in Montana are trying to compromise.

Montana's Blackfoot Valley is a 1.5 million acre watershed of thick forest and rolling fields. There are seven communities in it, about 35 ranches, thousands of cattle and at least a dozen packs of wolves. And in 2013, there were no confirmed livestock depredations to wolves -- a result, in part, of some community initiatives.

Jim Stone is the chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge -- a group of area landowners, ranchers, hunters, environmentalists and biologists working together to achieve shared goals.

Wolves will avoid new things, Stone says, even something as basic as a strip of flags demarcating territory -- or "flaggery." If Stone knows wolves are in the area, he'll string a perimeter around his or his neighbor's cattle in minutes.

"We believe that everyone can agree on 80 percent of the things in this valley. So we deal with those things on the front end ... Then we can deal with the other 20 percent, like wolves."

— Jim Stone, Chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge

There are other ways to keep wolves at bay -- like eliminating the bait. Traditionally, ranchers dispose of dead livestock in "boneyards" on their land. But Eric Graham spends part of his day collecting dead livestock from ranches in the area and taking them to an off-site compost area.

Learning New Tricks

Hilary Zaranek has experienced two sides. She volunteered with the Yellowstone Wolf Project before marrying into a ranching family in Montana's Tom Miner Basin. In Yellowstone, she saw how bison herded up when approached by wolves, instead of running like elk. So she's trying to teach her cattle to herd up like bison.

Hannibal Anderson, Zaranek's step-dad, lost livestock to wolves after their re-introduction. "Killing them doesn't do any good," he says. "They just come back."

That's why he's helping Zaranek train the cattle. In their first year, the cattle that were trained to herd suffered no losses.

It's a start.

— Hilary Zaranek
