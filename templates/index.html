Joyful Learning Environments

We spend so much time discussing how to create an effective or efficient classroom that we often overlook the foundation for all productive teaching and learning: joy.

Classrooms that nurture curiosity, discovery, and the open exchange of ideas are engaging. They provide a solid foundation for students to hone the skills essential to 21st-century success:

  • Critical Thinking
  • Communication
  • Collaboration
  • Problem Solving

In these talkative classrooms, students are motivated by peers who are well-qualified to engage each other and develop the intellectual values to appreciate others’ points of view. Gone are the days of rote memorization and hours spent quiet and stuck to a single, isolated desk or computer.

Caption

A happy, noisy classroom takes the best parts of childhood – the silliness, the spark, the itch to communicate and question – and uses them to the advantage and benefit of learning. Regardless of whether you teach STEM or ELA, 3rd grade or 12th, the elements of a boisterous learning environment can take root and flourish in your classroom.

These are the research-based practices that create happy, noisy classrooms:

1. Connecting with students

Every person leads a complex life – students and teachers alike. Everyone has unique interests, ideas, experiences, perspectives, and needs.

Caption

In a packed school day with many students and dwindling minutes, it’s even more important to build deeper connections with students by learning what drives, worries, and excites each and every one of them.

When you know each student’s interests and needs, you’re able to personalize instruction for them – and get them to the next level. You’re able to accomodate for gaps or bring in more rigorous and relevant materials. By deepening these connections with students, you establish a mutual respect and set the foundation for more inquiry and feedback later on.

Caption

What You Can Do

At the start of each year, make sure that you:
  • Can accurately pronounce all students’ names.
  • Are familiar with every student's home and family life.
    • Are they an only child?
    • Do they have siblings?
    • Do they speak a second language at home?
    • How do they get to and from school?
  • Know what interests each student.
    • What are their unique skills?
    • Are they passionate about video games? Sports? Books? History or math?
    • Which outlets spark their interest?
  • Understand what each student needs.
    • Is it helpful to have a moment of calm music and personal reflection before diving into coursework?
    • Does the student thrive with teacher or peer guidance?
    • How can their interests help facilitate the building of skills?

2. Personalized Feedback

As a teacher, you understand the importance of helping students recognize their errors. With the abundance of scantrons, apps, and automated scoring assessments, identifying errors can be efficient. Providing personalized feedback to correct them? Not as much.

Caption

It’s a timely endeavor, but it’s worth the effort to provide each student with personalized, direct, and specific feedback. A review of research by John Hattie found that among a sample of possible classroom influences, feedback from a teacher has the greatest effect on student achievement.

Hattie writes that teachers who “provide much more relevant, useful feedback” to their students are “better able to filter relevant from irrelevant information, and are able to monitor, understand, and interpret events in more detail… As a consequence they seek and provide more and better feedback in light of this monitoring” (pages 7-8).

Caption

What You Can Do

  • Make sure that the feedback you provide is:
    • Personal
    • Relevant
    • Clear
    • Actionable
  • Let students know that failure is okay – we all get things wrong sometimes. When students feel more comfortable sharing their thoughts, you’ll better understand where they’re at, and you’ll be able to provide more relevant feedback.
  • Iterate on your practice! Feedback goes both ways – don’t just provide feedback, solicit it from your students as well. You’ll get insights into how you can alter your teaching to be more effective.
  • Test out new solutions or strategies for helping students understand a topic. Feedback doesn’t always have to be a one-on-one discussion – it can be a new method of instruction, too.

3. Peer-to-Peer Discussion

Happy classrooms are noisy classrooms. They are classrooms in which students engage with peers to develop their ideas.

When we consider the skills our students need to achieve success in the 21st-century world, communication, collaboration, and critical thinking are chief among them. These are skills which cannot be honed within a silo.

Caption

By discussing and debating concepts with peers, students encounter and engage with different perspectives. They also learn how to advocate for their ideas, and are often motivated by their peers to do well too.

Research shows that in classes without discussion, “students achieved an average gain of only 25% between their pre- and posttest scores.” However, “when lecturers paused and asked students to discuss the concept presented in pairs or small groups (three or four students), students achieved an average gain of 48%” (PROPER CITATION TK).

Caption

Through peer discussion, students practice argumentation. They learn how to hold their ideas up to scrutiny and criticism, and they learn from their peers’ varied perspectives. By practicing argumentation, discussion, and debate, students gain a richer understanding of a subject and preparedness for the world awaiting them.

What You Can Do

  • Use a standard argumentation frameworks to help students develop their own critical understanding of what they’re discussing. In the CERCA framework, for instance, students start with a Claim, support it with Evidence, explain their Reasoning, and address Counterarguments while using Audience-appropriate language.

4. Cross-Curricular Collaboration

We know that cross-curricular collaboration is a highly effective strategy for improving learning—but it’s also a fun one. When students are able to witness the interconnectedness of what they’re learning, their engagement levels soar.

Caption

Anecdotally, many teachers know that learning deepens when students study a problem or topic through multiple lenses. On the research side, the benefits of cross-curricular collaboration are clear as well: A landmark University of Chicago study found that writing across subjects five or more times per month compared to less than five times per month had the single greatest impact on student achievement. The data also showed discussion, debate, and collaborative classroom activities practices like peer editing are the keys to success in all subjects.

What You Can Do

Use Project-Based Learning (PBL) to jump-start a cross-curricular collaboration plan. As part of PBL, students must analyze a problem and research potential solutions from multiple lenses: social and financial to name a few. Meet with your fellow teachers to see if there are any overlaps in topic areas that could work for a PBL unit.

6. Appreciation for Diversity of Perspectives

A happy classroom is a respectful classroom – plain and simple.

Eileen Murphy, founder and CEO of ThinkCERCA, says that “In an Engaging Classroom, peers who are well-qualified to engage each other and develop the intellectual values to appreciate others’ points of view.”

Caption

Students should be able to communicate openly, without judgment, in an instructional environment. This requires understanding, patience, and getting to know your students over time. Teaching across the curriculum incorporates a diversity of knowledge that gives your class the opportunity to investigate an issue from as many perspectives as possible.

7. Inquiry-Based Learning

Once a teacher has established a happy, interactive learning environment, the question becomes how to engageme each learner on a deeper level. The conditions have been set for children to think critically and retain what they’re learning, but how to best direct that energy?

Rather than delivering top-down lectures about a subject—the teacher as director and the students as information receptacles—an inquiry-based model is driven by questioning and dialogue. It’s not “Here’s how the American Revolution happened,” but instead, “What factors contributed to the American Revolution? How did it happen?” with the teacher playing the role of facilitator in the discussion.

What You Can Do

Lorem Ipsum
  • Follow a model and inquiry-based content pieces to use the educator’s knowledge and expertise without being prescriptive or inhibiting conversation. This Inquiry Design Model shows how to unite content with conceptual knowledge skills for the most critical thinking.
  • Think like our old pal, Socrates. Rather than telling students they are right or wrong, and ending the conversation there, lead students’ understanding through follow-up questions, reflection, and further inquiry. Thinking critically is thinking deeply.

Concluding Thoughts

For these happy classroom strategies to truly take root, it should be an entire community approach. Happiness has a ripple effect. Happy students lead to happy teachers lead to an ideal environment to grow in. We hope you take the ideas that we’ve shared with you here and spread them. A happy classroom for every student is our goal.
