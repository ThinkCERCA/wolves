People And The Wolf

For tens of thousands of years, wolves and humans have lived side by side. But can two territorial creatures at the top of the food chain peacefully coexist?

A Love-Hate Relationship

As far back as cave drawings, wolves have featured in human mythology. And it's been a love-hate relationship. There were fewer than 50 gray wolves left in the lower United States, most scientists say, as recently as a few decades ago. They had been hunted, trapped and poisoned to near extinction during the settling of the West.

Then human attitudes changed. Or enough of them, at least, to bring back the underdog. In the mid-1990s, the federal government transplanted 66 gray wolves from Canada to Yellowstone and Central Idaho to repopulate the region. [A line about the Yellowstone Wolf Project.]

Wolves became "the environmental movement poster animal," says Doug Smith, head of the Yellowstone Wolf Project, a group that studies and monitors wolves in the region.

The wolf population has since exploded. So, too, has a culture war. Today, there are nearly 1,700 gray wolves in the Northern Rockies. Good news to those trying to perpetuate the natural ecosystem; bad news to many who now inhabit it.

On The Lookout

Wolves are elusive. They go where they want. Enthusiasts who want to see them, let alone take pictures, have to hire guides or go to a wolf-viewing or rehabilitation center -- which is what we did to get our wolf photos in Yellowstone National Park.

Yellowstone has become known as the place to see wild wolves. They're like celebrities, with fan clubs and even Facebook pages. And people have become fiercely protective of them.

Members of the Yellowstone Wolf Project track the park's ten wolf packs, studying and monitoring their movements. They also help point tourists to the best places to see wolves. [A line about why they like wolves/why wolves matter/deserve protection.]

The Big Bad Wolf

If there's any truth to fables, wolves can be magical but even more menacing. Watching them through binoculars in Yellowstone is one thing. But about 95 percent of wolves live outside of the park -- near people -- where they kill livestock and game animals. In 2012, wolves killed at least 67 cattle and 37 sheep in the state of Montana. And far more elk.

Some people are taking action.

The main reason I hunt wolves is because I've seen what they do to other animals. They're killers. I want to do my part in managing them.

— Bruce Stell, outdoorsman and hunter

A Case Of Crying Wolf?

A big question is: Are wolves really to blame for the decline in elk populations? Montana Fish Wildlife and Parks is conducting a study in the Bitterroot Valley, near the Montana-Idaho border, to find out. [We need to know what he has concluded/found.]
Ben Jimenez is a field biologist with the Bitterroot Elk Research Project. The project got a lot of funding from area sportsmen who want to know what's killing the area's elk. He studies each carcass for signs of what did the killing.
Biologists placed radio transmitting tags in the ears of 84 elk calves late last spring. Each tag emits a different radio signal that Jimenez tunes into. The signal comes to a halt if the calf is dead, which is how Jimenez knows to go find them.

Adapting To The Landscape

Love them or hate them, it seems that wolves are here to stay. Wolf biologists say that at this rate, hunters couldn't eradicate wolves if they tried. They’re too elusive, too resilient. [Why were they able to the first time?]

Humans can keep trying. Or, other say, there's compromise.

Montana's Blackfoot Valley is a 1.5 million acre watershed of thick forest and rolling fields. There are seven communities in it, about 35 ranches, thousands of cattle and at least a dozen packs of wolves. And in 2013, there were no confirmed livestock depredations to wolves -- a result, in part, of some community initiatives.

Jim Stone is the chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge -- a group of area landowners, ranchers, hunters, environmentalists and biologists working together to achieve shared goals.
"Flaggery" is one technique ranchers use to spook off wolves. Wolves will avoid new things, Stone says, even something as basic as a strip of flags demarcating territory. If Stone knows wolves are in the area, he'll string a perimeter around his or his neighbor's cattle in minutes.

"We believe that everyone can agree on 80 percent of the things in this valley. So we deal with those things on the front end ... Then we can deal with the other 20 percent, like wolves."

— Jim Stone, Chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge
[Who's' "man's best friend" is this?]
Traditionally, ranchers dispose of dead livestock in "boneyards" on their land -- a tasty temptation for more predators. So Eric Graham spends part of his day collecting dead livestock from ranches in the area, removing a common draw for wolves.
Graham's job bridges a gap between conservationists and landowners. As part of the Blackfoot Challenge's Range Rider program, he tracks wolves in the area and reports his findings back to biologists and ranchers. It's like being a modern day shepherd on a motor-bike. "You got to build up that trust," he says.

I'd just as soon not have these wolves on the landscape. It would make my life a heck of a lot easier. But they are. ... We just got to deal with it. So that's where we're putting our efforts.

— Denny Iverson, rancher and landowner in the Blackfoot Valley.

Animal carcasses in the Blackfoot are brought to a compost site that's run by Montana's Department of Transportation. It's separated into two piles: Domestic animals and wild. Through November of last year, they had processed about 450 wild animals and 400 domestic.

"If we wait long enough as landowners to deal with issues like wolves, the government is going to come in and tell us how to do our business," Stone says. "Well we don't like that. So this valley says okay, let us deal with it and you can follow us."

Hilary Zaranek is trying to deal with the wolf issue in her own way. She volunteered with the Yellowstone Wolf Project before marrying into a ranching family in Montana's Tom Miner Basin. In Yellowstone, she saw how bison herded up when approached by wolves, instead of running like elk. So she's trying to teach her cattle to herd up like bison.

Hannibal Anderson, Zaranek's step-dad, lost some of the first livestock to wolves after their re-introduction. "Killing them doesn't do any good," he says. "They just come back." That's why he's helping Zaranek train the cattle.

The cattle that were trained to herd up when approached by predators suffered no losses in their first year. "It's a start," Zaranek says.
