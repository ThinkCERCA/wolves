People And The Wolf

Reported by Nathan Rott

Photography by David Gilkey

December 25th, 2013

For tens of thousands of years, wolves and humans have lived side by side.

But can two creatures at the top of the food chain peacefully coexist?

Near Extinction

As far back as cave drawings, wolves have featured in human mythology. And it's been a love-hate relationship. There were fewer than 50 gray wolves left in the lower United States, most scientists say, as recently as a few decades ago. They had been hunted, trapped and poisoned to near extinction during the settling of the West.

Then human attitudes changed. Or enough of them, at least, to bring the wolf back. In the mid-1990s, the federal government transplanted XX gray wolves from Canada to Yellowstone and Central Idaho to repopulate the region.

Wolves became "the environmental movement poster animal," says Doug Smith, head of the Yellowstone Wolf Project, a group that studies and monitors wolves in the region.

The wolf population has since exploded. So, too, has a culture war. Today, there are nearly 1,700 gray wolves in the Northern Rockies. The problems start when they stop respecting human territory.

Watching Wolves

Wolves are elusive. They go where they want. Enthusiasts who want to see them have to hire guides or go to a wolf-viewing or rehabilitation center, which is what we did to get our wolf photos in Yellowstone National Park.

Yellowstone has become known as the place to see wild wolves. They're like celebrities, with fan clubs and even Facebook pages. And people have become fiercely protective of them.

More than 3 million people visit Yellowstone National Park every year. Many to see the park's wolves. It's estimated that wolf-watching brings in roughly $35 million to the surrounding area.

Yellowstone Wolf Project

Members of the Yellowstone Wolf Project track the park's ten wolf packs, studying and monitoring their movements. They also help point tourists to the best places to see wolves.

The Wolf Problem

But wolves do cause problems. About 95 percent of them live outside of Yellowstone -- near people -- where they kill livestock and game animals. In 2012, wolves killed at least 67 cattle, 37 sheep, one dog, two horses and one llama in the state of Montana.

And far more elk.

The main reason I hunt wolves is because I've seen what they do to other animals. They're killers. I want to do my part in managing them.

— Bruce Stell, outdoorsman and hunter
There's a reason they call it hunting and not killing. Only around one-percent of hunters successfully kill a wolf.
In 2012, hunters and trappers killed a total of 225 wolves in Montana. The state sold nearly 19,000 licenses to kill them.

Elk Hunters

In parts of Montana, like the Bitterroot Valley, elk numbers have declined in recent years. For hunting outfitters, lower elk numbers have meant less business. And some hunters blame wolves.

Hunting outfitters, like these guys at Hell's A-Roarin' Outfitters, are like hunting guides. But since the re-introduction of wolves, Hell's A-Roarin' has started leading more horseback rides than hunting trips.
Hunting is best in the morning, says Warren Johnson, who owns Hell's A-Roarin'. He has his guides get up at 3 a.m. to ready their pack horses and load them up for the day. They want to be out in the woods, ready when daylight breaks.

It’s ruined our business here. Business we had in the family for over four generations.

— Bill Hoppe outfitter in Montana’s Paradise Valley.
Some people hunt for trophies. Others for food. In Park County, MT the median household income is about $40,000, so hunting can be a big part of people's livelihoods.

But do wolves really kill that many elk?

Montana Fish Wildlife and Parks is conducting a study in the Bitterroot Valley, near the Montana-Idaho border, to find out.
Ben Jimenez is the field biologist of the Bitterroot Elk Research Project. The project got a lot of funding from area sportsmen who want to know what's killing the area's elk. "Wolves have been a big issue down here," Jimenez says, "So there was some speculation from people that we were going to see a lot of wolf kills." Jimenez studies each carcass for signs of what did the killing.
Biologists placed little radio transmitting tags, like this, in the ears of 84 elk calves late last Spring. Each tag emits a different radio signal that Jimenez tunes into. The signal beeps at 40 beats a minute when the elk calf is moving. It increases to 80, if the tag hasn't moved in longer than three hours. "At this age, if it hasn't moved in three hours," Jimenez says, "it's probably dead." That's when he goes to find them.

Living with wolves

Love them or hate them, wolves are here to stay. Wolf biologists say that even with aggressive hunting seasons in Montana, people won’t be able to eradicate wolves. They’re too elusive, too resilient.

So the onus falls on people, to figure out how to live with them. And some are.

Montana's Blackfoot Valley is a 1.5 million acre watershed of thick forest and rolling fields. There are seven communities in it, about 35 ranches, thousands of cattle and at least a dozen packs of wolves. But in 2013, there were no confirmed livestock depredations to wolves thanks in part to some of the projects the community has started up.

Jim Stone is the chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge. It's a group of area landowners, ranchers, hunters, environmentalists and biologists that all work together under the same guiding principle: "We believe that everyone can agree on 80 percent of the things in this valley," Stone says. "So we deal with those things on the front end, build up that trust. Then we can deal with the other 20 percent, like wolves."
Flaggery is one tool ranchers use to spook off wolves. It's simple in design - flags attached to a single cord of electric wire - but it works. Wolves don't like new things, Stone says. So he's developed a way to put out flaggery in a matter of minutes, using a four-wheeler and a spool system. If he knows wolves are in the area, he'll string a perimeter around his or his neighbor's cattle.
Eric Graham spends part of his days collecting dead livestock from ranches in the Blackfoot. It's part of the Carcass Pick-up Program the community helped organize. Traditionally, ranchers would dump their dead livestock in what are called boneyards. "But that just sucks predators in," Graham says, which increases the chances of a conflict.
Graham's job is to bridge the gap between conservationists and landowners. As part of the Blackfoot Challenge's Range Rider program, he tracks wolves in the area and reports his findings back to biologists and ranchers. It's like being a modern day shepherd on a motor-bike. "You got to meet a lot of people and you got to get along with them," Graham says. "You got to build up that trust."

I'd just as soon not have these wolves on the landscape. It would make my life a heck of a lot easier. But they are. So it doesn't do me any good to go to the coffee shop or the bar and complain about it. We just got to deal with it. So that's where we're putting our efforts.

— Denny Iverson, rancher and landowner in the Blackfoot Valley.

Animal carcass's in the Blackfoot are brought to a compost site that's run by Montana's Department of Transportation. It's separated into two piles: Domestic animals and wild. Through November of last year, they had processed about 450 wild animals and 400 domestic.

"If we wait long enough as landowners to deal with issues like wolves, the government is going to come in and tell us how to do our business," Stone says. "Well we don't like that. So this valley says okay, let us deal with it and you can follow us."

Hilary Zaranek is trying to deal with the wolf issue in her own way. She volunteered with the Yellowstone Wolf Project before marrying into a ranching family in Montana's Tom Miner Basin. In Yellowstone, she saw how bison herded up when approached by wolves, instead of running like elk. So she's trying to teach her cattle to herd up like bison.

Hannibal Anderson, Zaranek's step-dad, lost some of the first livestock to wolves after their re-introduction. "Killing them doesn't do any good," he says. "They just come back." That's why he's helping Zaranek train the cattle.

The cattle that were trained to herd up when approached by predators suffered no losses in their first year. "It's a start," Zaranek says.
