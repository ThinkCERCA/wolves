This is a story about wolves

And about human beings. It's about what we, as two top predators, have in common -- we're social, adaptable and fiercely territorial. It’s also a story about whether we can get along.

People have been fascinated with wolves for centuries. They show up in our folklore and in our fairy tales. Today, in much of the American West, gray wolves also show up in our politics. I know this because I grew up in Montana, where wolves can be as important and divisive a topic as gun control or health care.

A few decades ago, wolves had been hunted, trapped and poisoned -- down to a population of about 50 in the contiguous United States. Then, in the mid-1990s, the federal government decided to bring them back, introducing 66 Canadian gray wolves into Idaho. They became "the environmental movement poster animal,” says Doug Smith, head of the Yellowstone Wolf Project, a group that monitors and studies wolves in Yellowstone National Park.

Biologist Doug Smith at Lamar Canyon in Yellowstone National Park, where some of the first gray wolves were re-introduced in 1995.

Caption

Since then wolves have thrived: Scientists say there are now about 1,700 of them. And so, after nearly 40 years on the endangered species list, the gray wolf may be removed from the list nationwide. In some places, like Montana, it has already been delisted.

Gardiner, Mont., is a small frontier-turned-tourist town at the north entrance to Yellowstone National Park -- and in the middle of the wolf debate.

Caption

So NPR photographer David Gilkey and I spent a few weeks in my home state with people on each side of the issue, and those in the middle. We found that oftentimes, the debate about wolves is not really about wolves at all.

Looking out for wolves

More than 3 million people from around the world visit Yellowstone National Park every year. The park is one of the best places in the world to see the gray wolf run and hear it howl. It's estimated that wolf-watching pumps about $35 million into the economies of communities around the park.

Hunting for wolves

But 95 percent of wolves live outside Yellowstone. That’s where a lot of the conflict happens. Wolves kill livestock -- and the elk that have for so long drawn hunters and tourists to Montana. State officials say wolves killed at least 67 cattle and 37 sheep in 2012, and many more elk.

Stell follows a set of fresh wolf tracks up a dirt road.

Caption

The main reason I hunt wolves is because I've seen what they do to other animals. They're killers. I want to do my part in managing them.

— Bruce Stell, outdoorsman and hunter
Wolf hunters like Stell had about a one percent success in previous years.

Caption

For cattle ranchers, it can add up: The death of one calf can mean a financial loss of about $1,000. Older cows cost even more.

For other people, it's more about the elk. In some parts of Montana, like the Bitterroot and Paradise valleys, elk populations are declining. Warren Johnson, who owns Hells A’ Roarin’ Outfitters, is one of many people who think that is because there are too many wolves.

A ranch hand prepares the horses for a hunting outing at Hell's A-Roarin' Outfitters, a small business near Gardiner, Mont., owned by Warren Johnson.

Caption

Guides at Hell's A-Roarin' Outfitters ready horses for the day's hunt.

Caption

The main target for hunters in the Yellowstone are elk. Some hunt for the meat, others for trophy.

Caption

The state of Montana has responded. To bring wolf numbers down, hunting quotas have been raised, and the cost of hunting licenses has dropped: Montana residents can kill up to five wolves every season, and the licenses are only $19 a piece.

But there’s a reason it’s called hunting and not killing: Only about 1 percent of wolf hunters actually succeed.

Studying wolves

So elk numbers have declined. But are wolves really to blame? The Montana Fish, Wildlife & Parks department is conducting a study in the Bitterroot Valley, near the Montana-Idaho border, to find out. The project got a lot of funding from area sportsmen who want to know what's killing the area's elk.

Field biologist Ben Jimenez studies elk carcasses to see which animal killed them. It’s kind of like wildlife CSI. Last spring, he and other scientists placed radio transmitting tags in the ears of 84 elk calves. Each tag sends a different radio signal that Jimenez tunes into. The signal gets faster if an animal dies, and when that happens, Jimenez heads out to find it.

Field biologist Ben Jimenez searches for an elk carcass in Western Montana's Bitterroot Mountains.

So far, the study has found that mountain lions are the primary killers of elk; bears come second; and wolves are a distant third. Still, the debate rages on.

Living with wolves

Montana's Blackfoot Valley is 1.5 million acres of thick forest and rolling fields. There are seven communities, about 35 ranches, thousands of cattle -- and at least a dozen packs of wolves. Surprisingly, though, in 2013 there were no reports of livestock depredations by wolves.

Ranchers in Montana's Blackfoot Valley are trying to find new ways of living with wolves.

Caption

Many scientists believe that, love them or hate them, wolves are here to stay. So some ranchers, hunters and conservationists in Montana are exploring new strategies that might allow wolves, people, elk and livestock to share the land and reach a better balance.

One thing they’ve learned: Wolves avoid new things -- even a basic strip of flags demarcating territory. It’s called “flaggery,” says Jim Stone, chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge, a coalition of landowners, ranchers, hunters, environmentalists and biologists.

Jim Stone, chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge, sets up a temporary fence on his cattle ranch to ward off wolves.

Caption

"We believe that everyone can agree on 80 percent of the things in this valley. So we deal with those things on the front end ... Then we can deal with the other 20 percent, like wolves."

— Jim Stone, Chairman of the Blackfoot Challenge
Seth Wilson works with Stone, and inspects the temporary fence as it's unrolled from a four-wheeler.

Caption

If Stone knows that wolves are in the area, he'll string flaggery around his or his neighbor’s cattle in minutes. It's not as permanent a solution as a gunshot, but he says it can work. Some of the people buying his beef want wolves around [meaning they're conservationists, or don't believe in killing them? I’m checking on this], he says, so to him, this is business.

Another thing they’ve learned to do: Eliminate the bait. Traditionally, ranchers dispose of dead cattle or sheep in "boneyards" on their land. That can draw scavengers and predators, including wolves. Eric Graham, who works for the Blackfoot Challenge, spends part of his day collecting dead livestock from ranches in the area and taking them to an off-site compost area.

The efforts seem to be working. More important, Stone says, they’ve kept the community working together.

The gray area

On a ranch in Montana’s Tom Miner Basin, Hilary Zaranek is taking her cattle to school. Before marrying into a ranching family, she had volunteered with the Yellowstone Wolf Project. On that job, she observed a few things.

She noticed how bison, when threatened by wolves, gather together in a tight herd and keep off predators as a group. So she’s trying to teach her cattle to do that, too.

Zaranek tries to herd up a few dozen yearlings.

Caption

Hannibal Anderson, Zaranek's stepfather, says killing the wolves doesn't do any good because “they just come back." And in their first year of training the cattle, they suffered no losses. It’s no silver bullet, though. Says Zaranek: “It’s a start.”

The politics of wolves in the West

In much of the American West, the gray wolf is a political issue as important — and divisive — as gun control, or health care or gay marriage are in other parts of the country. The federal government wants to remove the species from the endangered species list. NPR’s Nathan Rott spent weeks reporting from Montana, where wolves are no longer protected, about why these animals stir such deep emotions. Here’s his story.
